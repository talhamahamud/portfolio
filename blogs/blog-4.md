*Perhaps ten years from now, I will read this again and remember where I started.*

From the very core of my heart, I have always loved physics and mathematics. They are not just academic subjects to me—they are ways of understanding the universe itself. Every equation, every theorem, and every law of nature tells a story about how reality works. That curiosity is what drove me to pursue physics.

When the time came to choose a university, I had opportunities to study in other recognized institutions and in subjects that many people around me considered more prestigious or practical. But I didn't choose based on public opinion. In every admission application, my first choice was Physics and my second choice was Mathematics. Eventually, I joined Khulna University with one dream: to become a physicist.

However, after entering university, I encountered a reality I had never expected.

I found that much of the education system rewarded memorization rather than understanding. Instead of asking *why* Stokes' theorem works or *how* the curl operator arises naturally from vector calculus, students were often expected to memorize derivations and reproduce them in exams. Since examinations followed a predictable pattern, those who memorized efficiently usually performed well.

For someone driven by curiosity, this environment was frustrating.

In my first semester, I earned a CGPA of 3.25. It hurt deeply—not because of the number itself, but because I had come to university hoping to become a better physicist, yet I felt trapped in a system that valued recall over exploration.

In the second semester, I changed my strategy. I memorized more, focused on exam patterns, and my GPA improved to 3.75. But despite the better result, I felt strangely empty. I realized that I had gained grades without gaining the understanding I truly wanted.

So I returned to studying in my own way.

Over the following semesters, my results gradually declined: 3.74, 3.64, 3.33, 3.29, 3.04, and eventually 2.68 in my most recent semester. My cumulative CGPA now stands at 3.34.
![CGPA Graph](https://i.ibb.co.com/67ZH0hgL/cgpa.png)

Many people would see this as failure.

I don't.

Of course, I know I could have worked harder in some areas, and I continue to improve my discipline and consistency. But I also know that my time has often gone into chasing understanding rather than simply chasing marks. I have spent hours trying to grasp concepts that would never appear in an exam because I believe genuine knowledge lasts longer than a grade sheet.

Another challenge has been that initiatives to do something different or more ambitious have not always received encouragement. At times, it has felt as though bureaucracy and rigid expectations stand in the way of curiosity. That can be discouraging, but it has not changed my goal.

Today, I continue working on research projects, studying independently, and preparing for opportunities to study abroad. I still believe that the path to becoming a great physicist is built on persistence, curiosity, and a willingness to ask difficult questions.

This article is a reminder to my future self.

If you are reading this ten years from now, I hope you smile at the memory of these struggles. I hope you remember that there was once a student sitting in the back benches, worried about grades but unwilling to abandon the desire to truly understand the universe.

And if that student eventually became the physicist he dreamed of becoming, then every disappointment along the way was worth it.

I place my trust in Almighty Allah and continue moving forward with hope. My transcript does not define my future. My curiosity, perseverance, and faith will.

Insha'Allah, one day I will become an exceptional physicist and mathematician.

Until then, I will keep learning.
*My Grades Fell, But My Curiosity Never Did. To The Infinity And Beyond*
